Section ① · Digital Divide & Knowledge Gap

The Digital Divide

  • Not everyone has equal access to the Internet
  • "The developed world has a great advantage over the developing world"
  • "Access is not even" — uniform access remains a distant goal
  • This gap affects education, jobs, and life opportunities
Section ① · Digital Divide & Knowledge Gap

Why the Gap Grows: Two Models

Same media, different abilities → different outcomes. In the digital age, with no natural ceiling on knowledge, the gap may never close.

Section ① · Digital Divide & Knowledge Gap

The Consequences

  • Information inequality leads to opportunity inequality
  • The digital divide deepens existing social and economic gaps
  • Those already disadvantaged fall further behind
  • The advantage compounds — inequality breeds more inequality
Section ② · Violence, Passivity & Addiction

Media &
Violence

  • Kids are "susceptible to the negative images they see"
  • Repeated exposure leads to desensitization
  • Children may internalize a worldview where violence feels normal
  • Movies & games "may influence our world in ways we do not understand"
Section ② · Violence, Passivity & Addiction

Passivity &
Addiction

  • Passivity: TV turns us into observers, not participants
  • "They say it makes people too passive" — real activities replaced by screens
  • Addiction: "How long have I been wandering around cyberspace?"
  • Platforms are engineered to keep you scrolling — the addiction is a business model
Section ② · Violence, Passivity & Addiction

Wasting Time

  • Phone apps "may cause us to waste time" — a core chapter criticism
  • Social media platforms are designed to maximize engagement, not well-being
  • Some people spend up to 8 hours a day online — half of waking hours
  • "You shouldn't spend so much time connected to electronic devices"
Section ③ · Privacy, Data & Filter Bubbles

Privacy Invasion

  • "TV invades our privacy" — but digital media invades far more deeply
  • Every click, search, and scroll is tracked, recorded, and stored
  • Personal data becomes a commodity — bought and sold without consent
  • Most users don't realize the extent of what's being collected
Section ③ · Privacy, Data & Filter Bubbles

Filter Bubbles
& Echo Chambers

  • Filter bubbles: algorithms show only what we already agree with
  • Echo chambers: beliefs get reinforced, never challenged
  • The textbook warns media "encourages people to think alike"
  • Result: a "skewed impression of the world" — biased and inaccurate
Section ③ · Privacy, Data & Filter Bubbles

Brainwashed
by the Media?

  • Are we "brainwashed by the media" — forced to accept views without thinking?
  • Constant exposure to the same messages shapes our beliefs silently
  • We develop a "skewed impression of the world"
  • Critical thinking suffers when all inputs agree
Section ④ · Manipulation & Trade-offs

Advertising Overload

  • The textbook says media "has too much advertising"
  • Ads are everywhere — videos, feeds, search results, apps
  • Modern ads are personalized using your own data
  • Constant commercial pressure makes us materialistic
Section ④ · Manipulation & Trade-offs

The Trade-off
We Face

  • "There is a trade-off for whatever you do" — key chapter warning
  • We trade privacy for convenience — free services aren't free
  • We trade depth for breadth — more connections, shallower relationships?
  • We trade attention for entertainment — who really benefits?
Section ④ · Manipulation & Trade-offs

Media Interferes
with Life

  • Media "interferes with the way we spend our time"
  • "Cell-phone users babble on, ignoring the world around them"
  • We trade presence for distraction — half in, half out
  • The constant presence of screens changes how we relate
Conclusion

Technology — Blessing
or Curse?

The media is neither purely good nor purely bad. The key is awareness, balance, and conscious consumption.

"Technology — blessing or curse?" — Angeline A. Moscatt
